✦ The Loop Reflection — DESN368

The Loop
Reflection

Looking back at the quarter — what clicked, what didn't, and where this all goes from here.

01 — The Threshold

When did code actually click?

The moment code really clicked for me was when I got Flexbox to actually do what I was picturing in my head. I'd been fighting with layout for multiple of my projects for what felt like hours — things were stacking wrong, overflowing, just not cooperating — and then something about learning about justify-content and align-items suddenly made spatial sense. It wasn't just memorizing properties anymore; I could see the box model as a real thing, something I could manipulate with intention.

After that, I stopped guessing and started reasoning. That shift — from typing code and hoping to understanding what I was asking the browser to do — is what made the rest of the quarter feel different. Working with Claude in understanding how Flexbox works and how to use it made something click in my brain, and it helped me see code in a more linear way.

02 — The Pit

What was the hardest part?

The Field Guide assignment was genuinely hard for me. It required a level of editorial precision I wasn't used to — not just making something look nice, but structuring information with a clear hierarchy that actually guided a reader through content. Every time I thought it was close, something felt off: spacing, flow, the way text and image coexisted on the page.

Getting out of that pit meant taking a step back and treating the layout like a design problem instead of a code problem. Although I'm not even done with this project and still have a lot of work to do with it, it's still the project I'd most want to revisit, which means it taught me the most.

03 — The Pivot

Original vision vs. what I actually built.

To be honest, I never even completed the original Moodz & Vibes assignment, and therefore didn't have much to work off of with this project. However, earlier in the quarter when I began thinking about my future portfolio, I started with a cleaner, more conventional "design student portfolio" in mind — something polished and neutral. More professional and corporate.

But as I got further into the quarter and learned what was actually possible with CSS, I wanted the portfolio to feel more like me, not like what I thought a portfolio was supposed to look like. The dark, grungy, Tumblr-adjacent direction I landed on is genuinely the aesthetic I live in — it shows up in what I save, what I wear, what games I gravitate toward. Pivoting toward that felt less like breaking the plan and more like finally being honest about it. A portfolio should look like the person who made it, and this one does.

04 — The Process

What I'd do differently.

I'd start earlier. It sounds obvious, but I kept underestimating how long it takes to go from "I have an idea" to "this actually works in the browser." Every project had a moment where I ran out of time to finish what I'd started, and that's entirely on when I started.

I'd also ask for help sooner and read the documentation more patiently. I spent way too much time this quarter feeling stressed and overwhelmed, and that stopped me from excelling. If I could do this project again, or take the class again, I'd not let the anxiety stop me from creating.

05 — The Future

Design + code in my future practice.

I want to make video games, and knowing how the web actually works changes how I think about building anything interactive. UI design in games isn't that different from web design — both are about communicating information through visual hierarchy while someone is actively doing something else. Understanding the code side means I can actually implement what I design instead of handing it off and hoping.

This skillset also makes me a better collaborator. In a game development team, being the person who can speak both design and code — even imperfectly — is genuinely valuable. This course gave me a foundation I didn't have before, and I plan to keep building on it.
